## A0. Disambiguation — which business is this?

**The entity audited is the family-run open-air barbecue in the front yard of the Harris family home on Austin Conolly Drive, Gun Bay, East End, Grand Cayman — cooked under a large Neem tree.** It is the single "Big Tree BBQ" that appears on TripAdvisor (listing d6404824), the Cayman Islands Department of Tourism site, Cayman Compass, and its own Facebook page. There is no competing Cayman business of the same name, and the distinguishing details all corroborate one another:

- **The tree.** Every source describes cooking under one large tree (a Neem) on a residential front yard — the literal source of the name.
- **The owners.** Consistently named as **Henry Harris and his son Arvin Harris** across the Cayman Compass feature and multiple review write-ups.
- **The location.** East End / Gun Bay, Austin Conolly Drive — consistent across TripAdvisor, VisitCaymanIslands, Lonely Planet, Cayman Explorer and directory listings.
- **The format.** Order at the garage, sit under the tree, food brought out — described identically across independent reviews.

Identity is **confirmed**. No blending with unrelated "Big Tree" businesses elsewhere.

## A4. Customer sentiment & testimonials

**Rating:** **4.8 / 5 on TripAdvisor** across **~737 reviews** (as read July 2026); frequently ranked among the top BBQ / restaurants in the East End district. (Exact live count moves over time.)

**Recurring positive themes**
- **The ribs and chicken** are the standout — repeatedly called the best BBQ on the island; beef ribs singled out.
- **Authenticity** — real Caymanian food (turtle, conch, stewed beef) you can't easily get elsewhere.
- **The setting** — eating in the open air under the big shady tree with the ocean breeze is the experience people remember.
- **The people** — Henry and Arvin are warm, chatty hosts; visitors describe meeting locals.
- **Worth the drive** — reviewers from other districts and cruise/holiday visitors say the East End trip is worth it.

**Recurring caveats / "problems the site should solve"**
- **Getting there / knowing it's open** — it's remote, hours are limited (5 days), and the menu (esp. Sunday specials) changes. Visitors want to know *when to come and what's on before making the drive.*
- **Finding it** — a front-yard, roadside spot is easy to miss without a map/pin.
- **Cash** — casual roadside operations are often cash-first; unclear payment options frustrate first-timers.

## A6. Competitive landscape

East End has few sit-down options, so Big Tree competes less with restaurants than with "is it worth the drive vs eating closer to George Town / Seven Mile Beach." Reference points:

- **Vivine's Kitchen** (Gun Bay, East End) — the other iconic East End home-style Caymanian eatery; also famous, also cash/roadside, also **no strong dedicated website**. Big Tree's edge: barbecue + Sunday turtle. Differentiator opportunity: *a real website with hours, a map pin and a clear menu beats Vivine's near-zero web presence.*
- **Tukka** (East End) — a full-service restaurant with a website, menu and events; more polished web presence and card payment. Big Tree differentiates on price, authenticity and the under-the-tree experience — but should match Tukka on **basic web hygiene (hours, map, menu, click-to-call).**
- **General BBQ / local food stands island-wide** — mostly Facebook-only. A fast, mobile-first site with structured data would put Big Tree ahead on discoverability.

**Takeaway:** none of the direct comparators do e-commerce or online ordering. Big Tree does **not** need a cart. It needs the best *informational + conversion* site in its niche: hours you can trust, a map pin, a phone tap, and mouth-watering real photos.

## A7. Market context

- **Location/customer mix:** East End is the quiet, far end of Grand Cayman — a mix of locals (loyal, year-round) and tourists/day-trippers doing the East End loop (Blowholes, Wreck of the Ten Sails, diving). The site must serve both: locals want hours + Sunday specials; visitors want "why it's worth the drive" + a map.
- **Seasonality:** Cayman tourism peaks **mid-Dec–Apr** (high season) and dips in late summer/autumn (hurricane season, Jun–Nov). Sunday is the marquee day (turtle/seafood feast). Expect weekend and high-season surges.
- **Regulatory / cultural note:** **turtle** is a legal, traditional food in the Cayman Islands sourced through the regulated Cayman Turtle Centre / licensed supply — but it is a sensitive item for international visitors and must be described factually and respectfully, never sensationalised. Flag for owner sign-off on how it's presented.
- **Payments/logistics:** roadside operations are frequently **cash-first**; currency is KYD (pegged ~1 KYD = 1.20 USD, USD widely accepted). Confirm payment methods so the site can state them plainly.
