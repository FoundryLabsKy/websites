The people behind the pots

Cooked at home, the Caymanian way

Vivine's Kitchen isn't a restaurant that happens to be homey — it's a home that happens to feed you. And that makes all the difference.

The story of Vivine's Kitchen

At the far eastern tip of Grand Cayman, past the point where the tour buses turn back, the road runs out toward Gun Bay and the open Caribbean. That's where you'll find Vivine and Ray Watler — and their kitchen. There's no dining room in the usual sense. You order at the window of the family's own home, and the food comes out to picnic tables set under the sea-grape trees, with the surf a few steps away.

Vivine is a native Caymanian, and she cooks the island the way it has always been cooked: fish and conch pulled from local waters, goat curried low and slow, chicken browned down into rich gravy, cassava baked into dense, sweet heavy cake. Nothing is frozen in from far away for show. Whatever came in fresh that morning is what goes up on the blackboard — and when the blackboard's dishes run out, the kitchen's done for the day.

"A local gem that is an extension of Vivine's home. The food is homemade and delicious."

It's one of the last places on the island where you can still order traditional Caymanian turtle stew when it's in season, alongside conch, whelks and lobster as the year allows. There's no bar — just tall glasses of fresh tamarind, mango and sorrel juice, poured cold against the East End heat. And it's cash only, the way the old places always were.

People drive the better part of an hour from George Town and Seven Mile Beach to eat here, and they keep coming back for the same reason: it tastes like somebody's grandmother made it, because in spirit, somebody did. Travellers write that it's "like eating at Grandma's" and "a true taste of the Cayman Islands." That's not a marketing line Vivine came up with. It's just what the food is.

So come hungry, come with cash, and call ahead so Vivine knows to expect you. The drive east is part of the meal.
